HOW WE THINK: TECHNOGENESIS, MEDIA, AND THE RECONFIGURED MIND
TUE 10/21
[CORE INSIGHT: THE CO-EVOLUTIONARY THESIS]
Hayles's central premise: "We think through, with, and alongside media" (p.1). This signifies more than tool use; it's a profound claim about the co-evolution of human cognition and technologies (technogenesis). Our minds, now 'hybrid readers' (close/hyper/machine), are undergoing "epigenetic changes" (p.10) where media re-train our neural circuitry, impacting conscious, unconscious, and nonconscious perceptions.
[GUIDING INQUIRY: INTERVENING IN TECHNOGENESIS]
How do media's materialities and protocols, as 'writing-down systems,' actively "reengineer the conditions of everyday life" (p.13)? As "human agency becomes less a 'controller'... and more of a player among many 'influential forces'" (p.94), what are the implications for attention, labor, and our sense of the 'human'? How can we leverage Comparative Media Studies to devise "constructive interventions" (p.18) in this dynamic, technologically-catalyzed spiral of change?
[APPLIED CHALLENGE: MAPPING MEDIATED SHIFTS]
Using Hayles's framework, analyze one assigned 'artifact.' Map a concrete cognitive or labor transformation, tracing the "continuous reciprocal causality between human bodies and technics" (p.123). Consider the artifact's material instantiation (p.91), its media-specific affordances/constraints (p.26), and how it influences our reading strategies (p.78) or shapes the technological unconscious (p.96).
[READINGS: UNPACKING MEDIA, COGNITION, & TECHNOGENESIS]
[A] THEORETICAL FOUNDATION]
S19.A.1
Hayles, N. Katherine. How We Think: Digital Media and Contemporary Technogenesis. Chapter 1. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2012.
[READ: CLOSE, HYPER, MACHINE] [TECHNOGENESIS] [LEGOS]
  • Media as Co-evolutionary Partner: "We think through, with, and alongside media" (p.1).
  • The Passing Age of Print: Its practices now "visible as media-specific practices rather than the largely invisible status quo." (p.2)
  • Technogenesis: "epigenetic changes in human biology can be accelerated by changes in the environment that make them even more adaptive." (p.10)
  • Neural Plasticity: Brains "wired differently than children who grow up in other kinds of cultures" due to information-intensive environments (p.100).
  • Three Reading Modes: Close, Hyper, Machine reading are distinct yet interconnected "strategies to address complex problems." (p.26, 78)
  • Embodied/Extended Cognition: "all cognition is embodied... it extends beyond the body's boundaries." (p.17)
  • Comparative Media Studies: A framework for "exploring synergies between print and digital media." (p.7)
[B] CONTEMPORARY ARTIFACTS: AI & THE RECONFIGURATION OF LABOR/AGENCY]
S19.B.1
Grey, C. G. Humans Need Not Apply. YouTube, 2014.
[WATCH: AUTOMATION, LABOR & THE TECHNOLOGICAL UNCONSCIOUS]
  • Automation's Epigenetic Impact: How AI reconfigures the human labor environment, forcing new "biological adaptations" (p.100) and cognitive shifts.
  • The Technological Unconscious in Labor: The video depicts how "actions, expectations, and anticipations that have become so habitual they are 'automatized,' sinking below conscious awareness" (p.96) shape our relationship with machine labor.
  • Defining the 'Human': What remains unique to human cognition and labor as machines achieve increasing sophistication?
S19.B.2
Cheng, Ian. Life After BOB: The Chalice Study / Thousand Lives. Art Installation & Simulation, ongoing.
[EXPLORE: DISTRIBUTED AGENCY & NARRATIVE/DATABASE SYMBIOSIS] [THOUSAND LIVES] [LIFE AFTER BOB] [1000 FUTURES]
  • Generative AI & Distributed Authorship: How Cheng's simulations instantiate "multi-agent interactions" (p.13) and challenge the "distributed author function" (p.236), blurring lines of creation.
  • Narrative vs. Database: Explore the "limits of symbiosis" (p.175) when machine-generated 'stories' meet human interpretation, questioning how "database models how minds think and how the world works" (p.179).
  • Complex Temporalities: Consider how these autonomous, evolving digital lives manifest "complex temporalities enfolding past into present, present into future." (p.86)
[C] HISTORICAL TECHNOGENESIS: PRE-DIGITAL CONTEXTS OF CODE & LANGUAGE]
S19.C.1
Hallner, Andrew. The Scientific Dial Primer: Containing Universal Code Elements of Universal Language, New Base for Mathematics, Etc. San Francisco: Sunset, 1912.
[INTERACT: EARLY TECHNOTEXTS & THE SPATIALIZATION OF TIME]
  • The Technotext as Artifact: Hallner's Primer functions as a historical "technotext" (p.106), revealing pre-digital "technogenesis in action" (Ch.5 title) and early human-technical hybridity.
  • Code & Universal Language Dream: Analyze the tensions between the "proto–universal language" (p.158) of the Dial and its ideological underpinnings, anticipating modern debates on coding's "neutrality" versus its embedded values.
  • Spatialization of Time: How this device represents an early attempt to "capture time" (p.111) through spatialized practices, and what this tells us about the "place of the human" (p.123) in mediated communication.
  • Materiality of Inscription: Examine the Dial's physical form as a "writing-down system" (p.236) that shaped cognitive practices before digital media.
